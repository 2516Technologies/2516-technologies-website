Why 2516

Fifteen years of building systems that can't afford to fail.

Before there was 2516 Technologies, there was a long career building data infrastructure for organizations where the stakes were real where a broken audit trail meant a failed FDA inspection, where a data error meant a misallocated defense budget, where the wrong number in a financial model meant a regulatory violation.

I believe in applying my craft to work that matters. Everyone wants a new AI toy. The real challenge and the real responsibility is building AI in organizations where the consequences of getting it wrong are measured in people's lives, not quarterly metrics.

The background

Regulated industries taught me what maturity looks like.

My career was built in the spaces where data infrastructure has real consequences. Department of Defense financial systems. FDA-regulated pharmaceutical labs. SOX-compliant financial modeling for publicly traded companies. These aren't environments where you ship fast and fix later. They're environments where you design for correctness first, and where the audit trail matters as much as the application itself.

I spent years building data warehouses, audit tools, and financial models for clients who couldn't afford to get it wrong organizations where a compliance failure meant fines, where a data breach meant congressional testimony, where a single miscalculation could cascade into something much larger.

That work shaped how I think about software. Every system I build starts with the question: what happens when this breaks, and who is affected? That discipline is rare in the AI era, where the pressure to ship fast has become the dominant instinct. It's the instinct I've spent 15 years resisting.

The conviction

The nonprofit sector deserves the same quality of work.

There's a conversation that happens in the technology industry that I've always found troubling: the idea that quality infrastructure is something you earn that you have to grow large enough, or raise enough capital, before you deserve the kind of systems that actually hold up under pressure.

Nonprofits don't operate in that framework. A food bank running on broken technology isn't a startup learning from failure it's a community going without. An education nonprofit with a data breach isn't disrupting the market it's exposing vulnerable people. The stakes are different, and the standards should reflect that.

AI has changed the economics of what's possible. For the first time, the kind of expert guidance that required a six-figure consulting engagement can be delivered at scale to organizations that have never had access to it. That's not a product opportunity it's a responsibility. And Orr Group is the right organization to carry it.

Why this project

This isn't a client engagement. It's a conviction.

I've spent a career building systems for organizations that could pay for quality. The work was meaningful, and the results were real. But there's always been a question in the background: what about the organizations that do equally important work and can't access the same quality of infrastructure?

The answer, for most of technology's history, has been: they make do. They patch together consumer tools. They hire whoever they can afford. They operate on foundations that were never designed for what they're trying to do.

This platform changes that answer. For the first time, the same standard of work I've applied to defense contractors and pharmaceutical companies can reach a nonprofit with eight employees and a mission that matters. That's worth building. That's worth building well.
